What Is Amendment 5 and Why Should Anyone Who Cares About Public Education Pay Attention?

Video coming soon.

There is a constitutional amendment headed to Missouri's August 2026 ballot called Amendment 5. If Missouri voters approve it, it becomes part of the Missouri Constitution. The measure began in the legislature as House Joint Resolutions 173 and 174 (HJR 173/174). Here is what it would do: set Missouri on a path to eliminate the state individual income tax completely.

That Sounds Good. What's the Catch?

Paying less state income tax sounds good; for many Missouri voters, that may be the entire appeal.

If less money leaves your paycheck, you have more control over what you do with it. That is an easy argument to understand.

So why are people nervous?

Because eliminating a tax does not eliminate the public costs that tax currently pays for.

Amendment 5 changes where the money — in this case public education money — comes from, or whether the money shows up at all.

On the surface Amendment 5 may sound like merely a tax policy question. In Missouri, that also makes it a public education question.

How Missouri Pays for Public Schools

Missouri funds public schools through a system called the foundation formula. Passed by the legislature in 2005, the formula was designed to do two things: ensure that every district has enough money to provide an adequate education, and level the playing field between districts with strong local property tax bases and those without.

That second part matters. Property taxes vary enormously across Missouri. The median home value in some districts is $44,500. In others it is $613,300. Missouri's own school funding task force has acknowledged these inequities.

The state formula is supposed to be the equalizer. It is the systemic mechanism that is supposed to keep a child in a poor rural district or a low-income urban neighborhood from receiving a fundamentally different education than a child in a wealthy suburb.

In Missouri, the state income tax is the primary source of money that funds that educational formula. See the FY2027 Executive Budget.

"Phase-out the individual income tax based on revenue growth." — Official ballot language, Missouri Secretary of State, Amendment 5 (2026) · sos.mo.gov
"State and local sales and use taxes (or any similar transaction-based tax) may be expanded by legislation to impose taxes on transactions involving any goods and services." — Amendment 5 text, as reported by KCTV5
"Require any increase in sales tax rates or expansion of the sales tax base be offset by a reduction in the income tax rate." — Official fair ballot language, Missouri Secretary of State, Amendment 5 (2026) · sos.mo.gov
The amendment does not specify which services will be taxed, what rates will apply, or require a public vote on those decisions. Lawmakers may expand sales taxes within a five-year window without returning to voters. — KCTV5: What the ballot language leaves out

The amendment creates the framework. The legislature would still decide many of the operational details later.

What the Amendment Does Not Say

The amendment does not include a detailed replacement tax plan.

For example, if Missouri currently funds such a large percentage of its public schools through income taxes, how can those schools be sure they can pay their teachers in a few years in a competitive teacher market?

What This Means for Schools That Are Already Struggling to Meet Students' Needs

The foundation formula is supposed to protect districts that cannot generate enough local revenue. Research from Forward Through Ferguson documents how funding inequities in Missouri continue to fall along racial and economic lines. When the state's share shrinks, wealthy districts absorb the loss from their local property tax base. Poor districts do not have that cushion.

Missouri voters are being asked to eliminate the state's primary school funding mechanism — while it is already underfunded — without a defined replacement plan.
