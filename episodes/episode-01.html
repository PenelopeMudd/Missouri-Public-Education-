What Is HJR 173/174 and Why Should Anyone Who Cares About Public Education Pay Attention?

Video coming soon.

There is a constitutional amendment moving through the Missouri legislature right now. In August when it goes on your ballot, if you vote yes, it becomes part of the Missouri Constitution. The amendment is called HJR 173/174. Here is what it does: it sets Missouri on a path to eliminate the state individual income tax completely.

That Sounds Good. What's the Catch?

Paying less state income tax sounds good; for many Missouri voters, that may be the entire appeal.

If less money leaves your paycheck, you have more control over what you do with it. That is an easy argument to understand.

So why are people nervous?

Because eliminating a tax does not eliminate the public costs that tax currently pays for.

HJR 173/174 changes where the money — in this case public education money — comes from, or whether the money shows up at all.

On the surface HJR 173/174 may sound like merely a tax policy question. In Missouri, that also makes it a public education question.

How Missouri Pays for Public Schools

Missouri funds public schools through a system called the foundation formula. Passed by the legislature in 2005, the formula was designed to do two things: ensure that every district has enough money to provide an adequate education, and level the playing field between districts with strong local property tax bases and those without.

That second part matters. Property taxes vary enormously across Missouri. The median home value in some districts is $44,500. In others it is $613,300. Missouri's own school funding task force has acknowledged these inequities.

The state formula is supposed to be the equalizer. It is the systemic mechanism that is supposed to keep a child in a poor rural district or a low-income urban neighborhood from receiving a fundamentally different education than a child in a wealthy suburb.

In Missouri, the state income tax is the primary source of money that funds that educational formula.

What the Amendment Says

In plain language, HJR 173/174 would change the Missouri Constitution to create a pathway for eliminating the state individual income tax over time.

To make that possible, it authorizes lawmakers to expand sales and other transaction-based taxes instead.

It also ties parts of that phaseout process to future revenue growth, meaning the transition would not happen all at once.

In other words: the amendment creates the framework. The legislature would still decide many of the operational details later.

What the Amendment Does Not Say

The amendment does not include a detailed replacement tax plan.

For example, if Missouri currently funds such a large percentage of its public schools through income taxes, how can those schools be sure they can pay their teachers in a few years in a competitive teacher market?

It does not list which goods or services would be taxed. If Missouri expands taxes on "services," does that mean your haircut? Your plumber? Your mechanic? The amendment does not say which services will be taxed. Additionally, the amendment does not ask voters to approve that future list — those decisions would be made later by the Missouri legislature in Jefferson City.

It does not specify final tax rates. If the state shifts from taxing income to taxing transactions, what rates would replace that revenue? The amendment does not say.

It does not explain exactly how lost state revenue would be replaced. Fox C-6 expected roughly $142 million in operating revenue for the 2025–2026 school year. School districts make real commitments based on those projections. If state funding becomes unstable during a tax transition, what fills that gap? The amendment does not say.

What This Means for Schools That Are Already Behind

The foundation formula is supposed to protect districts that cannot generate enough local revenue. Research from Forward Through Ferguson documents how funding inequities in Missouri continue to fall along racial and economic lines. When the state's share shrinks, wealthy districts absorb the loss from their local property tax base. Poor districts do not have that cushion.

Missouri voters are being asked to eliminate the state's primary school funding mechanism — while it is already underfunded — without a defined replacement plan.
