Too Louse Rock — Episode 1

What Is HJR 173/174 and Why Should Anyone Who Cares About Public Education Pay Attention?

Video coming soon.

There is a constitutional amendment moving through the Missouri legislature right now. If it passes the legislature, it goes on your ballot. If you vote yes, it becomes part of the Missouri Constitution — and constitutional changes are very hard to undo.

The amendment is called HJR 173/174. Here is what it does: it sets Missouri on a path to eliminate the state individual income tax completely.

That may sound like a tax policy question. It is also a public education question.

How Missouri Pays for Public Schools

Missouri funds public schools through a system called the foundation formula. Passed by the legislature in 2005, the formula was designed to do two things: ensure that every district has enough money to provide an adequate education, and level the playing field between districts with strong local property tax bases and those without.

That second part matters. Property taxes vary enormously across Missouri. The median home value in some districts is $44,500. In others it is $613,300. Some districts have double the operating funds per pupil compared to others. Local levies range from $2.75 to nearly $6.50 per $100 of assessed value. Missouri's own school funding task force has acknowledged these inequities.

The state formula is supposed to be the equalizer. It is the mechanism that keeps a child in a poor rural district or a low-income urban neighborhood from receiving a fundamentally different education than a child in a wealthy suburb.

The state income tax is the primary source of money that funds that formula.

What the Numbers Look Like Right Now

In FY2026, the foundation formula is funded at approximately $4.2 billion. Missouri spends roughly $7,100 per student in state funding annually through the formula.

But here is the thing: the current budget already falls $190 million short of what the formula requires by law. The legislature knew about the gap. The Senate declined to address it. School officials are already worried.

Missouri is starting this conversation in a hole.

One more piece of context: Missouri already provides among the lowest state-level education funding in the country as a percentage of total revenues. Only New Hampshire is lower.

What the Amendment Does and Does Not Say

The amendment proposes eliminating the state income tax and replacing it with an expanded sales tax. Here is the exact language:

"…state and local sales and use taxes (or any similar transaction-based tax) may be expanded by legislation to impose taxes on transactions involving any goods and services."

Any goods and services. The list — what gets taxed, at what rate, and who pays — gets decided later by the legislature, without another public vote. Lawmakers have a five-year window to work it out.

Eliminating the income tax creates an estimated $9 billion hole in the state budget. Without significantly expanding what gets taxed, filling that hole would require raising the sales tax rate to nearly 13%.

No state has ever made this switch. Every state that currently has no income tax — Florida, Texas, Nevada — was built that way. They did not eliminate an existing income tax and replace it with a sales tax. Missouri would be the first.

What This Means for Schools That Are Already Behind

The foundation formula is supposed to protect districts that cannot generate enough local revenue. Research from Forward Through Ferguson documents how funding inequities in Missouri continue to fall along racial and economic lines. When the state's share shrinks, wealthy districts absorb the loss from their local property tax base. Poor districts do not have that cushion.

Missouri voters are being asked to eliminate the state's primary school funding mechanism — while it is already underfunded — without a defined replacement plan.

That is what is on the table.
