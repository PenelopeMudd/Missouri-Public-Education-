# Chronic Absenteeism in U.S. Schools
## State of the Evidence — Seminal Documents

*Limits-of-what-is-known framing. Not exhaustive.*

---

## The Definitional Shift

**Chronic absenteeism** = missing 10% or more of enrolled school days (typically 18+ days in a 180-day year).

This is distinct from **average daily attendance (ADA)** — the traditional metric, which masks individual student patterns by reporting school-level averages. A school can have 95% ADA while 20% of its students are chronically absent, because the high-attenders absorb the calculation.

The shift from ADA to chronic absenteeism as the primary metric is itself a research contribution. Chronic absenteeism is an individual-level measure. ADA is not.

---

## The Foundational Document

**Balfanz, R. & Byrnes, V. (2012). *Chronic Absenteeism: Summarizing What We Know from Nationally Available Data.* Johns Hopkins University Center for Social Organization of Schools.**

[Full report — Everyone Graduates Center](https://new.every1graduates.org/wp-content/uploads/2012/05/FINALChronicAbsenteeismReport_May16.pdf)

The first national-scale analysis. Estimated 10–15% of U.S. students (5–7.5 million) were chronically absent using data from 6 states. Identified high-poverty urban districts reporting rates as high as 33%. Established the 10% threshold as the field standard. Shifted policy conversation from "who is truant" to "who is disappearing from school incrementally."

**What it established:** Chronic absenteeism is a systemic pattern, not an individual behavior problem. High-poverty schools are disproportionately affected.

**What it could not establish:** Causality. Whether absenteeism causes lower outcomes or is a symptom of the conditions that also cause lower outcomes remains unresolved in this document.

## Pre-Pandemic Baseline

National chronic absenteeism rate: approximately **15% (2018)**.

Source: [U.S. Department of Education Civil Rights Data Collection](https://www.ed.gov/teaching-and-administration/supporting-students/chronic-absenteeism)

---

## The COVID Rupture

National rate surged from **15% (2018) → 28% (2022)** — nearly doubling in four years.

By 2021–22: **66% of enrolled students** attended a school with high or extreme chronic absence levels.

Racial/economic disparities widened:
- Asian students: 16% chronically absent (2022)
- White students: 24%
- Hispanic students: 36%
- Black students: 39%

Source: [Attendance Works — Rising Tide of Chronic Absence](https://www.attendanceworks.org/rising-tide-of-chronic-absence-challenges-schools/)

---

## Post-COVID: Not Recovering

**Malkus, N. (2024). *Long COVID for Public Schools: Chronic Absenteeism Before and After the Pandemic.* American Enterprise Institute.**
[AEI Report](https://www.aei.org/research-products/report/long-covid-for-public-schools-chronic-absenteeism-before-and-after-the-pandemic/)

**Malkus, N. (2025). *Lingering Absence in Public Schools: Tracking Post-Pandemic Chronic Absenteeism into 2024.* AEI.**
[AEI Report — PDF](https://www.aei.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/06/Lingering-Absence-in-Public-Schools-Tracking-Post-Pandemic-Chronic-Absenteeism-into-2024.pdf)

By 2023: rates improved in 33 of 39 reporting states — but remained **75% above pre-pandemic baseline**.

By 2024–2025: approximately **22% of U.S. students** still chronically absent. In roughly half of urban districts, more than **30%** of students are chronically absent.

**Key framing from Malkus:** This is not a recovery. It is a new baseline.

Source: [RAND — Chronic Absenteeism Still a Struggle in 2024–25](https://www.rand.org/pubs/research_reports/RRA956-34.html)

## Student-Reported Reasons (2024–25)

From RAND American Youth Panel:
- Illness: 67%
- Feeling down or anxious: 10%
- Oversleeping: 9%
- Uninterested in attending: 7%

**What this cannot tell us:** Whether "illness" is acute illness, chronic condition, mental health symptom, or avoidance coded as illness. The self-report categories are not clinically defined.

## Brookings — Depth and Persistence

**Brookings Institution (2024). Student-level attendance patterns show depth, breadth, and persistence of post-pandemic absenteeism.**
[Brookings Article](https://www.brookings.edu/articles/student-level-attendance-patterns-show-depth-breadth-and-persistence-of-post-pandemic-absenteeism/)

Individual student-level analysis confirms the post-pandemic pattern is not driven by a small group of severely absent students. The distribution shifted — more students are missing more days across the board.

## What the Research Has Not Resolved

1. **Causality direction.** Chronic absenteeism correlates with lower achievement, higher dropout rates, and economic disadvantage. Whether absenteeism drives outcomes or is a downstream indicator of conditions that also drive outcomes is not established.

2. **Mechanism.** "Illness" is the dominant self-reported reason. Whether this reflects actual illness rates, health infrastructure gaps, mental health crisis, or avoidance behavior is not systematically distinguished in national data.

3. **Intervention evidence.** Research on *what works* to reduce chronic absenteeism is thin relative to the descriptive literature. Most intervention studies are small-scale, short-term, or site-specific.

4. **Post-pandemic floor.** Whether the current elevated rate (22%) is a temporary plateau or a permanent reset is unknown. Two years of partial recovery followed by stabilization does not yet distinguish between trajectory and new normal.

5. **School-level vs. system-level causes.** The data identify where absence is concentrated. They do not cleanly separate what is attributable to school climate, neighborhood conditions, family capacity, health infrastructure, or policy environment.

## Summary — Starting Point

Chronic absenteeism (missing 10%+ of school days) affects an estimated 22% of U.S. students as of 2024–25 — down from a pandemic peak of 28% in 2022, but 75% above the pre-pandemic baseline of 15%. In high-poverty urban districts, rates exceed 30%. Racial and economic disparities are pronounced and widened during COVID.

The foundational research (Balfanz & Byrnes, 2012) established chronic absenteeism as a systemic pattern rather than an individual behavior problem, and shifted the national metric from average daily attendance — which masks individual disappearance — to individual-level absence rates.

The post-pandemic data indicate the elevated rates have not recovered. Two years of partial improvement have stabilized, but whether the current rate represents a trajectory or a new floor is not yet known.

**What the research does not resolve:** Causality between absenteeism and academic outcomes runs in both directions and is not cleanly separated. Student-reported reasons (67% illness) are not clinically defined. Intervention evidence is thin. National data cannot distinguish school-level from system-level causes.

The descriptive literature is robust. The intervention and causal literature is not.
